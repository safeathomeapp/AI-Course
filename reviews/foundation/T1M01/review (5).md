## Section 2: Concept Explanation

**Rating: 4/5**

The six-step mental model — Prompt → Context Window → Probability Engine → Output → Human Review → Decision — is genuinely excellent. I love a good mental model and this one is *tight.* I've explained this concept to my team before but in a much more ad hoc way. Having it as a linear sequence like this is something I'm definitely going to steal — erm, borrow — for my own team documentation.

The "fluency is not evidence" line is CHEF'S KISS. That's quotable. I screenshotted that immediately.

As someone who's been using AI for 18+ months, the hallucination section wasn't new to me, but the framing — "predictable result of how language models operate under uncertainty" rather than a random bug — is actually more precise than how I've been explaining it. I usually just say "sometimes it makes stuff up, always double-check." This is a more sophisticated explanation and I can see how it'd help people understand *why* rather than just *what.*

The "what AI is strong at" and "what AI is weak at" breakdown was good but felt a little... safe? Like, of course AI is good at first-pass drafting. I use it for way more complex thngs than that. The strong/weak framework is right directionally but it undersells how much you can push AI once you understand its limits.

Hot take: The course positions AI as this carefully-to-be-managed tool, which is correct, but I think there's a risk of being so cautious in the framing that you undercook people's ambition for what's actually possible. The best AI users I know aren't just using it for "first-pass drafting" — they're building entire content engines with it.

The prompt framework section (Role/Context/Task/Constraints/Output Format) is solid. This is *exactly* the kind of structure I've been developing with my team — though I've been calling it slightly different things. Nice to see it codified. Would have loved to see a sixth element for Tone/Voice, which makes a huge difference in marketing outputs specifically, but that's probably Module 2 territory.

## Section 3: Demonstrations

**Rating: 4/5**

The onboarding example (operations/HR) was fine but wasn't really my world. I read it, got the point, moved on.

The P&L example hit closer to home — I'm often in the weeds with our marketing finance data and have actually used AI to help summarize campaign performance reports for leadership. The "do not invent numbers" constraint is something I wish was in every finance prompt I've ever written. I've definitely had outputs that felt a little... *optimistic* about what the numbers showed. Not wrong exactly, just... generous in interpretation. Good reminder.

What I really wanted was a *marketing* example in the demonstrations. I know there's one later in the real-world application section (more on that shortly), but the demonstrations section itself stuck to ops and finance. For a course that presumably wants to appeal to marketers, content teams, and growth professionals, leading with operations and finance feels like a missed opportunity to immediately signal "yes, this is for you too."

The before/after structure of the demonstrations is super effective though. Seeing the weak prompt vs. the improved prompt side-by-side makes the improvement instantly obvious. This is actually how I explain prompting to my team — show them both, make the gap visible.

One thing I noticed: the improved prompts are MUCH longer than what most people actually write day-to-day. There's a learning curve there that the course sort of glosses over. Going from "give me a campaign for our spring offer" to a five-section structured prompt takes practice. Worth acknowledging.

## Section 4: Guided Exercise

**Rating: 3/5**

I'll be transparent: I did this pretty quickly. Like 10-12 minutes. Because I already have a library of prompts I've been refining for months — I basically skimmed the instructions, grabbed a prompt I'd already improved, ran both versions for comparison purposes, and it confirmed what I already knew: structured prompts produce better outputs.

The exercise itself is well-designed for someone who *hasn't* done this before. The evaluation checklist (Clarity / Structure / Assumptions / Missing Information / Usefulness for Decision Making) is actually a pretty rigorous framework. I might use that checklist format when I'm reviewing my team's AI outputs.

What didn't land for me: the exercise felt designed for someone starting from zero. When you're already using structured prompts daily, "rewrite a vague prompt" isn't a revelatory exercise. I'd love a harder version — something like "here's a structured prompt that's still producing mediocre outputs, what's wrong with it?" That's the actual skill gap for intermediate users.

For the target audience (people new to structured prompting), I think this exercise works. But there's no stretch version for people who are past the basics.

Pro tip for the course designers: consider adding a "Level 2" version of the exercise for learners who are already familiar with structured prompting. Even if it's optional.

## Section 5: Real-World Application

**Rating: 5/5**

Okay THIS is where I got genuinely engaged. The marketing campaign ideation scenario is *exactly* my world.

The Version A vs. Version B breakdown — vague "give me a campaign for our spring offer" vs. the full structured prompt with audience, channel, risks — is so good. And I'm not going to pretend I don't sometimes write Version A prompts when I'm rushing. I do. We all do. But seeing the comparison made explicit like this is a good gut-check.

The human review process at the end of this scenario is solid:
1. Validate claims
2. Check audience assumptions against real data
3. Adapt to brand voice
4. Stakeholder review

This is *exactly* what a responsible workflow looks like. As someone who's been scaling our content operations... I'll admit this checklist is more thorough than what we currently have documented. We do this intuitively but we haven't formalized it, which means when I'm handing off to my team, they're not always clear on what "review" means. This gave me an idea for a team process document I want to build.

The line "human review protects quality, compliance, and accountability" is framing I want to use in a presentation I'm building for our next team all-hands. Genuinely useful.

One thing I'd add: what happens when the AI-generated campaign concept is *technically correct* but tonally wrong for your brand? That's the gap I run into constantly. Brand voice alignment isn't just a human-review step — it's a constraint that's hard to encode in a prompt and often requires a dedicated revision pass. Would love a section on this.

## Section 7: Emotional Learning Experience

**Rating: 4/5**

Mostly this felt like a validation exercise for me — confirming that I've been thinking about AI the right way. That was satisfying.

But I'll be honest about one moment that gave me slight pause: the common beginner failure pattern section.

> "User asks a vague question. AI gives a confident answer. User assumes correctness. Output is used without verification. Error appears later in a business workflow."

I know this pattern. I've seen it in my team. But reading it laid out like that... there was a moment of "okay, when's the last time I did this?" Not often — but it's happened. Especially when I'm moving fast between projects and don't have time to properly verify.

The framing here is good — it's not alarmist, it's practical. But I think for more experienced users there's a slightly uncomfortable reckoning in that section. Which is probably the point.

One thing the course does well emotionally: it doesn't treat AI with suspicion or fear. The tone throughout is "here's how to use this well" rather than "here's why you should be scared of this." That's the right energy. AI isn't dangerous — misuse is the issue. The course gets that.

The marketing scenario made me feel like the course actually understood my job, which matters more than I expected.

## Section 10: Weakest Part

The exercises are clearly designed for beginners, and there's no pathway for learners who are past that stage. I get it — this is T1M01, Foundations — but even a brief acknowledgment of "if you're already using structured prompts, here's a harder version" would have been welcome. Right now it feels like the course assumes all learners start from zero.

Also, and I say this as someone in marketing: the demonstration examples skew heavily toward operations and finance. Two out of two demonstrations. The real-world application is marketing, which helps, but if you're going to put examples in the demonstrations, one marketing-adjacent example would have signalled more clearly who this course is *for.*

The "what AI is strong at" and "what AI is weak at" sections feel a bit conservative to me. First-pass drafting and summarizing are the floor, not the ceiling. I'd love to see a more ambitious framing of AI capabilities alongside the honest limitations. Underselling the upside doesn't serve practitioners.

## Section 11: Missing Content

**Things I would genuinely add:**

1. **Brand voice as a specific challenge.** This is THE issue for marketing teams using AI at scale. Generic quality-check steps aren't enough — there should be a dedicated section on brand voice maintenance when using AI for content production. This is a real gap in current AI training content and it'd differentiate this course.

2. **Intermediate-level exercise variant.** Optional, but valuable for users who are past the basics.

3. **Prompt iteration as a skill.** The course shows you one structured prompt and compares it to one vague prompt. In reality, getting great output usually takes 2-4 iterations. A quick note on iteration strategy — when to refine vs. when to restart — would be genuinely useful.

4. **AI tool comparison.** The optional advanced section mentions comparing outputs across two AI tools, which is great. But a brief note on when you'd choose ChatGPT vs. Claude vs. Gemini would be immediately actionable for practitioners. Even a one-liner on "different models have different strengths" would be useful context.

5. **The risk of *over*-reviewing.** This is a bit counterintuitive but real: in a fast-moving marketing environment, the review step can become a bottleneck if it's not scoped correctly. When is a quick skim review appropriate vs. full verification? Would love a risk-calibrated review guide.

## Section 12: Final Evaluation

**Overall Rating: 4/5**

**Would you recommend this course to a colleague?** Yes — with one caveat: I'd recommend it specifically to colleagues who are newer to AI or who haven't thought systematically about their AI workflow. If someone's already been using AI heavily for 12+ months, they might find the foundation content moves slower than they'd like. But the structured prompt framework and the portfolio artefact make it worth their time regardless.

**Who specifically would benefit most:**
- Marketing teams just starting to integrate AI into their workflows
- Managers who want to create AI governance for their teams
- Anyone who's been using AI casually and wants to professionalize their approach

**Is the course worth the investment?** Yes. Especially if your company is expensing it (which mine is). The ROI is real — even one workflow improvement from the portfolio artefact alone saves hours per week. The structured prompt framework is immediately applicable and if it reduces one AI-generated mistake making it to a client or leadership presentation, it's already paid for itself.

**Final thought:** The course doesn't try to be everything, which I respect. It sets a clear scope — this is the foundation, here's the mindset, here's the framework — and delivers on that promise. What I'd love to see is a Level 2 course that assumes you've mastered this and goes deeper. I'd be first in line for that.

Already wrote a LinkedIn post about the structured prompting framework. It got good engagement, which tells me this content resonates beyond just me.

## Reviewer Summary

Strong foundation module with a clean mental model, a genuinely useful prompt framework, and one excellent marketing-relevant scenario. As someone who's been in the AI space for a while, some of the content was review — but the structured articulation of concepts I've been using instinctively is valuable in itself, and the portfolio artefact is immediately usable. The course earns four stars for its clarity, its practical framing, and the "fluency is not evidence" insight that I suspect will be genuinely revelatory for less experienced users. Drops one star for the lack of any intermediate pathway — learners past the basics are left without a challenge track — and for the demonstrations leaning too heavily into operations/finance when the real-world application section proves the course can speak marketing fluently. If you're newer to AI or building structured workflows for a team, this is an excellent starting point. If you're already deep in it, you'll move quickly but still walk away with a few sharp tools you can actually use.
