# 2. Concept Explanation

**Did the explanation help you understand the concept?**

Yes. The core concept — that AI output requires human review before use in decisions — is communicated clearly and with appropriate repetition. The explanation builds logically from the mental model through the hallucination mechanism to practical strengths and weaknesses, ending with the structured prompt framework. The progression is coherent.

From an instructional design perspective, I would describe this as a well-structured expository section. The challenge is that expository text alone has limited transfer value without embedded checkpoints. I will expand on this in my Comments.

**Did any terminology feel confusing?**

Two terms are introduced without adequate plain-language definition:

- **"Context window"** — used in the mental model as if self-explanatory. The phrase "what is in scope" is offered as a definition, but this is circular. A learner without prior AI exposure would not know what "in scope" means in this technical context. A one-sentence plain-language definition is needed: something along the lines of "the context window is the total information available to the model in a given session — think of it as the AI's working memory for that conversation."

- **"Tokens"** — appears in the hallucination section with no definition. This is a technical term with a specific meaning (roughly, the units of text a language model processes, which may be whole words, partial words, or punctuation). Leaving it undefined creates a comprehension gap for non-technical learners without adding anything to the conceptual point being made. Either define it or use "words" as a plain-language approximation.

At scale across 2,800 employees, undefined technical terms create disproportionate friction. Learners who encounter an unexplained term either stop and search externally (breaking flow) or continue with an incorrect mental model (undermining transfer). Neither outcome is acceptable at enterprise level.

**Did the mental model make sense?**

Yes. The six-step model (Prompt → Context Window → Probability Engine → Output → Human Review → Decision) is well-constructed. It is memorable, logical, and — critically — it explicitly names "Human Review" as a step rather than an assumption. This is the correct design decision. Making the review step visible in the model reinforces it as non-optional.

I would note that the model as presented is linear. In practice, AI workflows are often iterative — learners will prompt, review, refine the prompt, and prompt again. Acknowledging this iteration loop, even briefly, would more accurately represent the actual workflow and would better prepare learners for real use.

**Was the explanation too long, too short, or appropriate?**

The overall length is appropriate for a foundation module. Section-level balance varies:

- Strengths/weaknesses lists: well-calibrated, scannable, actionable
- Hallucination explanation: slightly dense; the conceptual point is important but could benefit from a concrete example
- "Why this tool is suddenly everywhere": the shortest section, but arguably the least necessary for this learning objective — this content reads more as orientation narrative than directly enabling the stated outcomes

Rating (1–5):
Concept clarity: 4
Terminology accessibility: 3
Usefulness of mental model: 5

**Additional L&D Assessment:**
Scaffolding quality: 3 — The section delivers content without formative checks. There is no knowledge-check question, embedded reflection prompt, or checkpoint before moving to demonstrations. For a learner population that includes complete novices, this creates a risk: they advance to the next section without verifying that core concepts have landed.

**Comments:**

The conceptual foundation is solid. My primary concern from an instructional design standpoint is the absence of formative assessment within this section. Industry best practice for online self-paced content recommends at minimum one embedded knowledge check per major concept. A two-to-three question check here — testing comprehension of the mental model and the failure pattern — would accomplish three things: confirm learner understanding before progression, provide learner confidence (or prompt re-reading), and give us as administrators early signal on concept acquisition.

The "common beginner failure pattern" and its corrected version are among the strongest content elements in the module. This is concrete, specific, and — from a learning design perspective — creates a memorable contrast that improves retention. The contrast format (failure pattern vs. correct pattern) is effective pedagogical design and should be used more throughout the course.

I also want to note that the section makes no reference to regulatory or compliance contexts. Our organization operates in financial services, which is highly regulated. The principle of human review is directly connected to our regulatory obligations under SEC, FINRA, and internal compliance policies. The course does not acknowledge that for some industries, human review is not just good practice — it is legally required. An addendum or a brief callout noting regulated industry considerations would significantly increase relevance for our learner population and others like it.

A customer service representative reading these demonstrations may not recognize their own work tasks in either scenario.

Yes — the contrast is unambiguous and pedagogically effective. The before/after format is a strong design choice: it externalizes the difference rather than describing it abstractly. The learner can see, not just hear, why the structure matters. This is good instructional design.

The constraint language in both improved prompts ("do not invent numbers," "do not invent legal compliance steps") is appropriately specific and risk-focused. For our regulated industry context, this framing is exactly right. The three-point breakdown for the finance example (blocks fabricated numbers, separates facts from interpretation, creates format for decision meetings) models the kind of analytical thinking we want learners to apply when they design their own prompts. This is implicit transfer scaffolding — learners see the reasoning applied, which helps them apply it independently.

**Could you imagine applying this approach in your own work?**

Yes, and I have already done so. The structured prompt framework maps directly to how I approach AI for course outline development. Seeing it formalized confirms and refines my existing practice.

For our learner population at large, applicability will vary significantly by role. I would expect high applicability for analytical, managerial, and communications roles, and lower applicability for operational and customer-facing roles without supplementary examples.

# 4. Guided Exercise

**Was the task easy to understand?**

Yes. The task description is clear and the steps are numbered. The instruction to run both prompts and compare outputs using the evaluation checklist is specific enough to prevent ambiguity about what completion looks like.

**Did the instructions feel clear?**

Clear for a confident learner with an existing work context and access to an AI tool. My assessment for enterprise deployment identifies three gaps:

1. **No worked example.** The module provides a partial worked example (the finance report prompt breakdown) but does not show the learner what a completed exercise looks like — including both the vague and structured prompt text, and a filled-in evaluation checklist. Without this model, learners who are uncertain will not know if they are completing the exercise correctly. Based on our previous training deployments, the absence of a worked example typically reduces completion rates by 15-25% among novice learners.

2. **No guidance for learners without immediate AI tool access.** The instructions say "run both prompts in the same AI tool." Approximately 35% of our workforce does not have an established AI tool account. The module does not address how to create one, which platform to use, or what to do if access is unavailable. This is a significant onboarding gap that would need to be resolved with supplementary materials.

3. **Evaluation checklist calibration.** The five-point evaluation criteria are well-chosen but subjective without anchor descriptions. What does a "5" look like for "Clarity: Is the response easy to understand?" versus a "3"? Without calibration examples, learner ratings will vary widely, making the exercise difficult to assess at scale.

**Did the evaluation checklist help you compare outputs?**

Yes — the structure is sound and the criteria are appropriate. "Usefulness for Decision Making: Can a manager act on this safely?" is particularly strong. It grounds evaluation in downstream accountability rather than subjective quality perception.

My recommendation: add anchor descriptions for each rating level on each criterion, and provide a completed example checklist as a reference. This would both improve calibration and increase learner confidence.

**How long did the exercise actually take?**

I completed the exercise in full: 35 minutes. This included writing both prompts, running them in ChatGPT, completing the evaluation checklist, and writing responses to the three follow-up questions. I selected a task from my own work context (drafting a communication to business units about a new compliance training requirement).

I would estimate that a novice learner unfamiliar with AI tools would require 45-60 minutes for this exercise, and might struggle without the worked example and access guidance noted above.

Rating (1–5):
Instruction clarity: 3
Exercise usefulness: 4
Difficulty level: 3 (Moderate — appropriate for foundation level, but scaffolding is insufficient for novice learners)

**Additional L&D Assessment:**
Scaffolding for diverse learners: 2 — The exercise works well for learners who are confident, have relevant work context, and have AI tool access. For learners without one or more of these conditions, the exercise presents significant barriers to completion.

**Comments:**

The exercise is conceptually well-designed: it operationalizes the module's core learning objective and produces a tangible output learners can reference. The evaluation checklist is a good tool. My primary concern is the lack of scaffolding for learners who are not already confident in their ability to perform the task.

For enterprise deployment, I would need to see: (1) a worked example showing a complete exercise with filled-in checklist, (2) guidance on AI tool setup or access, (3) at least two to three backup scenarios for learners who cannot apply the exercise to their own work context, and (4) calibration anchors for the evaluation criteria.

I would also recommend adding a facilitator note suggesting that managers or team leads review exercise outputs with learners in a brief team discussion. This increases transfer and creates accountability without requiring significant facilitation time.

# 5. Real-World Application

Yes, as a business scenario. The marketing campaign use case is well-constructed and the before/after comparison is effective. My evaluation concern is the same as for the demonstrations: one scenario, one domain, one role type (marketing manager).

I would flag one gap for our regulated industry context: the review process does not include a compliance or legal review step. For a financial services organization, any AI-generated content that will be used externally or in regulated contexts should include legal/compliance sign-off. **Did it help connect the concept to real professional work?**

For learners whose work resembles the scenario, yes. For others, it requires active transfer. Active transfer is a learner responsibility but should be supported by instructional prompting — a brief "how might this apply in your role?" reflection question following the scenario would facilitate this.

# 6. Portfolio Artefact

**Does the artefact feel useful outside the course?**

Yes — and this is one of the module's strongest instructional design decisions. The portfolio artefact produces an observable, applicable output that is immediately deployable in a professional context. An "AI Tool-Use Operating Checklist" has genuine utility as a governance document for responsible AI use, which aligns well with our organizational priorities.

From an instructional design perspective, portfolio artefacts that have real-world application significantly outperform purely academic assessments in both learner motivation and knowledge retention. This is a deliberate and well-justified design choice.

**Could you realistically use this in your job or portfolio?**

Yes. I can see deploying an adapted version of this artefact as a standard template for our organization — part of a broader "Responsible AI Use" governance framework that we are in the process of developing. The five-field structure (Task Type / Risk Level / AI Role / Required Human Verification / Final Decision Owner) maps directly to the kind of role-based guidance our business units need.

The portfolio value for individual learners is also clear. This is a tangible deliverable that demonstrates not only AI competence but responsible AI practice, which is the right signal for a foundation-level credential.

**Was the template sufficient to create the artefact?**

The template is a solid starting structure. I have two instructional design recommendations:

1. **Provide a worked example row.** Without an example showing what a completed entry looks like, learners — particularly those less confident in their own judgment — will be uncertain whether their entries are "correct." A single completed row with an annotation explaining the reasoning would reduce this uncertainty.

2. **Provide guidance on risk classification.** "Risk Level (Low / Medium / High)" is listed as a field but the module provides no rubric for making this classification. What distinguishes a Low-risk AI task from a Medium-risk one? Without classification criteria, learner entries will be inconsistent and the artefact will not be comparable across a cohort. This is a significant gap for enterprise deployment where we would want to assess artefact quality at scale.

Rating (1–5):
Practical usefulness: 5
Portfolio value: 4

**Additional L&D Assessment:**
Assessment rigour: 3 — The artefact is a strong assessment vehicle in concept, but the absence of a worked example and risk classification rubric reduces its reliability as a consistent measure of learner competence.

**Comments:**

I want to emphasize that the inclusion of a portfolio artefact as the module's primary deliverable is the right instructional design choice. It aligns with Kirkpatrick Level 3 evaluation (behaviour) rather than Level 1 (reaction) or Level 2 (learning), which is the relevant level for assessing whether training actually changes how employees work. This design decision demonstrates instructional sophistication that I do not see in many competitor offerings.

For enterprise deployment, I would work with the vendor to develop: (1) a worked example row for the template, (2) a risk classification rubric, (3) a facilitated discussion guide for managers to review artefacts with their team, and (4) a brief assessor checklist so that L&D staff can evaluate artefact quality consistently across a large cohort.

# 11. Missing Content

**Did the module assume knowledge you did not have?**

For me personally, no — with the exception of the terminology gaps I noted. For our learner population, yes, in two respects:

1. **Technical terminology** (context window, tokens): Needs brief plain-language definitions
2. **AI tool access**: The module assumes learners have an AI tool account and know how to use it at a basic level. No setup guidance is provided. For first-time users, this is a significant gap. A "Getting Started with AI Tools" resource — even a one-page reference sheet — would address this.

**If yes, what additional explanation would help?**

Beyond the two points above, the module would benefit from the following additions for enterprise deployment:

1. **Pre-assessment (2-3 questions):** Baseline knowledge check before content begins. Allows learners to calibrate engagement level, provides administrators with pre-training data.

2. **Formative checks within Section 2:** Embedded knowledge questions after the mental model and after the failure pattern sections. Confirms comprehension before learner advances.

3. **Facilitator guide:** A separate document with session timing guidance, discussion prompts for cohort-based delivery, and notes on managing diverse learner populations. Essential for any organization delivering the course in a facilitated format.

4. **Alternative exercise scenarios:** Two to three additional worked contexts for learners whose roles do not map to the module's existing examples (customer service, healthcare, education, retail).

5. **Industry-specific compliance addendum (optional):** For regulated industries (financial services, healthcare, legal), a supplementary note on how regulatory requirements interact with the module's human review framework. This would be optional for general audiences but important for our context.

6. **Post-assessment (3-5 questions):** Closing knowledge check to confirm learning gain. Essential for Kirkpatrick Level 2 measurement and for completion certification.

# Reviewer Summary

T1M01 demonstrates genuine instructional design quality in its content foundation: the learning objectives are measurable and appropriately scoped, the mental model is memorable and pedagogically sound, the before/after demonstration format is effective, and the portfolio artefact is the strongest assessment choice I have seen in any of the courses I am currently evaluating for enterprise deployment. What should definitely stay unchanged is the core instructional design philosophy: the "tool not authority" framing, the explicit inclusion of human review as a non-optional workflow step, the before/after contrast format in the demonstrations, and — above all — the portfolio artefact as the primary module deliverable, which reflects a Kirkpatrick Level 3 orientation that is unusual and valuable in this market.
