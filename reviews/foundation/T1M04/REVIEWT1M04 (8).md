**Does the tone feel approachable, professional, confusing, or overly technical?**

# 2. Concept Explanation

**Did the explanation help you understand the concept?**

Yes. The five-part prompt structure (Role, Context, Task, Constraints, Output Format) is explained with the right level of detail — each element is defined, given a failure-mode justification, and later illustrated in demonstrations. From an instructional design standpoint, this is appropriate scaffolded introduction before application.

**Did any terminology feel confusing?**

"Output schema" appears once in the weak-prompt patterns list but the module uses "Output Format" everywhere else. This is a terminology inconsistency that should be resolved for clarity across learner profiles.

"Downstream rework" is professional jargon that will be accessible to most business learners but may need clarification for less experienced staff.

**Did the mental model make sense?**

Yes, and the failure-mode mapping is the module's most significant instructional design achievement. Teaching each element in terms of what it prevents — rather than just what it is — activates learner motivation by connecting the concept to observable problems they have already experienced. This is consistent with problem-centered instructional design (Merrill's First Principles) and it works.

**Was the explanation too long, too short, or appropriate?**

Appropriate. The concept section is well-calibrated in length. The subsections are well-delineated and each serves a distinct instructional purpose.

Rating (1–5):
Concept clarity: 5
Terminology accessibility: 4
Usefulness of mental model: 5

Comments:

The "prompt design as workflow design" framing is instructionally significant. It elevates the concept from individual skill to organizational process — a shift that is critical for enterprise adoption. If prompting is a personal writing technique, it stays with individuals. If it is a workflow interface, it becomes an organizational standard. That reframe has direct implications for how we position this training in our firm's AI literacy curriculum.

The five weak-prompt pattern examples are well-chosen and will resonate with learners who have experienced AI output quality issues. Connecting the abstract concept to recognized failure patterns before the exercise is sound pedagogical sequencing.

# 4. Guided Exercise

**Was the task easy to understand?**

Yes. The exercise is well-designed: select a real prompt, rewrite it with the five-part structure, run both versions, evaluate with the checklist. The inclusion of an example output (product analyst assistant with user interview summaries) helps calibrate expectations.

**Did the instructions feel clear?**

Yes, with two instructional design observations:

First: the instruction to "run both versions in the same AI tool" presupposes tool access that may not be universal in enterprise training environments. For corporate deployment, learners may need guidance on which tool to use or confirmation that they have access to a practice environment.

Second: the evaluation checklist lacks exemplars. Scoring from 1-5 on "structural usability" or "assumption transparency" requires judgment that newer AI users may not yet have. Two or three scored examples — showing what a 1, 3, and 5 would look like on "structural usability" — would reduce evaluator variance and improve the exercise's validity as a learning assessment.

**Did the evaluation checklist help you compare outputs?**

Yes. Five criteria plus three reflection questions is well-scoped. The criteria align with the module's learning objectives.

**How long did the exercise actually take?**

Approximately 45 minutes — including selecting a real prompt from my work, building the structured version, testing both, and completing the evaluation.

Rating (1–5):
Instruction clarity: 4
Exercise usefulness: 5
Difficulty level: 3

Comments:

From a learning effectiveness standpoint, this exercise is the strongest in the course. Testing a real prompt from your actual workflow produces immediate, personally relevant evidence of the concept's value — this is active learning at its most effective. The contrast between structured and unstructured output is not abstract; it is personal and concrete.

For enterprise deployment, I plan to facilitate this exercise as a team workshop, with each function group selecting prompts relevant to their domain and comparing outputs. The collaborative version will surface team-specific insights that the individual version cannot.

# 9. Most Valuable Part

The failure-mode mapping for each prompt element, combined with the "prompt design as workflow design" reframe.

From a training design standpoint: teaching an element by naming the problem it solves is more effective than teaching it as an arbitrary component of a formula. The failure-mode justification is what converts a five-item checklist into a problem-solving framework. That distinction determines whether learners remember and apply the structure or treat it as a procedural requirement they fulfill and forget.

---

# 10. Weakest Part

The evaluation checklist lacks exemplars. For a self-assessment tool measuring relatively abstract qualities (assumption transparency, constraint compliance), numerical scores mean different things to different raters. Without anchored examples showing what a 1, 3, and 5 look like on each criterion, the 1-5 scale produces inconsistent self-assessments that don't enable meaningful comparison or progress tracking.

This is a standard instructional assessment design issue: all rubrics should have performance level descriptions, not just criterion labels.

---

# 11. Missing Content

Module is well-scoped and self-contained.

For enterprise L&D and regulated industry deployment:

1. **Evaluation checklist anchors.** As above — performance level descriptions for each criterion.

2. **Team deployment guidance.** How do you build and maintain a team-level prompt library? What review and maintenance cycle is appropriate? How do you ensure consistency across team members' prompt use?

3. **Domain-specific prompt examples for financial services.** For our deployment context, examples in compliance documentation, client communication, and lending support are necessary supplements.

4. **Chaining preview.** The module mentions prompts will be chained in later modules. A brief description of what chaining means would help learners see the curriculum trajectory and understand why the single-prompt discipline they're building here matters as a foundation.

5. **Tool access guidance for the exercise.** For learners without established AI tool practice environments, guidance on tool selection and access would prevent a practical barrier.
