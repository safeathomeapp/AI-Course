# Course Module Feedback - Patricia Morrison - R03

**Module:** T1M02 – How Large Language Models Actually Work

---

## Section 1: First Impression

**Rating: 2/5**

I will be honest with you — when I saw the title "How Large Language Models Actually Work," my first thought was that this module was not meant for someone like me.  I am an executive assistant.  I do not need to understand how a language model works any more than I need to understand how a car engine works to drive to the grocery store.

I almost skipped this module entirely and asked my manager if I could move on.  I am glad I did not, or at least, I am partly glad — I will explain that below.

The opening of the module did not help with my anxiety about it.  Words like "tokenization" and "probability ranking" appeared in the first few paragraphs and I found myself reading them over and over trying to figure out if they were terms I should know.  I did not know them.  I wrote them down on a notepad and looked them up.

I think if the module had opened with something like "Have you ever wondered why AI sometimes gives you a confident answer that turns out to be completely wrong?" I would have felt much more at home.  That is a question I know the answer to from my own experience.  Starting with the technical vocabulary felt like starting a recipe by explaining the chemistry of baking.

---

## Section 2: Concept Explanation Quality

**Rating: 2/5**

I am going to be candid here, and I hope this is not taken as a criticism of whoever wrote this material.  I am sure it is very good for people who have a technical background.  For me, personally, it was quite difficult.

**Tokenization:**  I read this section three times.  On the third read, I think I understood that the AI breaks words into smaller pieces before processing them.  I think the analogy somewhere in there about word chunks was trying to help, but I still am not sure I fully grasped why this matters for how I use AI in my daily work.  What would have helped me: something like "think of it like autocomplete on your phone, but working at a much larger scale."  My phone autocomplete is something I use every day.  If you start there, I can follow the rest.

**Probability Ranking:**  This is actually the section that made the most sense to me, once I got to it.  The idea that AI is choosing the most likely word rather than the correct word explains something that has bothered me for months.  I asked Copilot to help me draft a letter to a vendor and it included a detail about our firm that was completely fabricated — a project we had never done.  I did not understand why it would do that.  Now I do, or at least I think I do.  It was guessing based on patterns.  That explanation was genuinely helpful.

**Context Window:**  I understood this concept better than the others, perhaps because the analogy to "not being able to see the whole document" is quite intuitive.  I have experience with this — I sent Copilot a very long meeting transcript once and the summary it gave me left out the last hour of discussion.  Now I understand why.

Overall I would say: the concepts are valuable, but the language assumes too much prior knowledge.  I needed simpler entry points throughout.

---

## Section 3: Demonstrations/Examples

**Rating: 2/5**

I need to be honest that the software development demonstration was essentially meaningless to me.  I recognize that is not a fair criticism of the demonstration itself — I simply do not have the background to understand code review scenarios.  I sat through it and tried to follow along, but I felt quite lost.

The finance demonstration was more accessible, though still challenging.  The scenario involving a financial report with missing data being "filled in" by AI with fabricated numbers — that I understood.  I have helped prepare board reports for senior leadership.  The idea that AI might invent a confident explanation for a financial result when it doesn't have all the information is genuinely alarming to me.  I made a note to be more careful when I use Copilot to help summarize financial documents.

What I would have found most helpful: a demonstration involving everyday office work.  Something like AI helping draft a professional email when given incomplete information about the situation, and then confidently including details it made up.  Calendar management, correspondence, meeting summaries — these are the tasks I do with AI.  A demonstration in that context would have made the lesson far more personal and memorable.

I am giving this section a 2 out of 5 because one of the two demonstrations was inaccessible to me, and neither demonstration fully reflected my work context.

---

## Section 4: Guided Exercise

**Rating: 2/5**

I found this exercise quite stressful, I will confess.  The task of comparing an "unconstrained prompt" versus an "uncertainty-aware prompt" required me to understand both of those terms well enough to construct them — and I was not entirely confident I did.

I tried the exercise.  I asked Copilot to help me draft a response to a client inquiry where I intentionally left out some details about what the client had asked.  The first version gave me a confident draft that assumed details I hadn't provided.  The second version, where I told Copilot I wasn't sure about part of the situation, did ask me a clarifying question before proceeding.  I think that is what was supposed to happen?

I was not entirely certain I had done the exercise correctly.  There was no example of a completed exercise to compare my work against, which is something I really depend on when learning new things.  I often learn best by seeing a finished example and then working backwards.

Also, I want to note: I am 52 years old and I did not grow up with this kind of interactive digital learning.  I prefer having clear written instructions I can print out and follow step by step.  The exercise instructions felt a bit open-ended for my comfort level.

---

## Section 5: Real-World Application

**Rating: 3/5**

This section was actually the most encouraging part of the module for me, which I was not expecting.

The guidance on how to prompt AI when you know you have incomplete information — being explicit about what you don't know, asking AI to flag assumptions rather than fill them in — that is something I can apply immediately.  I draft a lot of correspondence on behalf of my executive, often without having full context about the relationship or situation.  This module gave me a specific strategy for those moments.

I also appreciated the reminder that the Human Review step is non-negotiable.  That is consistent with what I learned in Module 1 and it is a principle I have already started applying.

I would have liked to see a few more office administration examples.  The applications described were mostly aimed at knowledge workers with more technical roles.  But the principles were clear enough that I could extrapolate.

---

## Section 6: Portfolio Artefact

**Rating: 3/5**

The LLM Reliability Control Card.  I will be honest: when I first saw this, I panicked a little.  Another form to fill out.  Another template with technical-sounding fields.

But once I read through the instructions and understood what each field was asking, I found it was actually quite manageable.  I completed it for an email drafting task — the kind of thing I do every day.

I do have one piece of feedback: the field labels assume a level of comfort with AI concepts that not everyone completing this course will have.  "Required Constraints" and "Human Verification Owner" are phrases that a senior technical professional will understand immediately.  For someone like me, a brief example of what to write in each field would be enormously helpful.  Even just one completed example card would have saved me quite a bit of uncertainty.

I am giving this a 3 because I was able to complete it and I think the framework is genuinely useful.  It just needed more hand-holding for non-technical users.

## Section 7: Emotional/Learning Experience

**Rating: 2/5**

I want to be careful here because I do not want my personal anxieties about technology to unfairly color my evaluation of material that may be perfectly appropriate for others.

But I did find parts of this module quite stressful.  The opening technical sections made me feel out of my depth in a way that was discouraging.  There were a few moments — particularly in the tokenization section — where I genuinely considered closing the module and telling my manager the content was too advanced for me.

What kept me going was the finance demonstration and the probability ranking explanation, both of which connected to things I have actually experienced with AI.  Those moments of recognition — "oh, THAT'S why it does that" — were valuable and worth pushing through for.

I ended the module feeling cautiously more informed.  Not confident, exactly, but better equipped than I was before.  I think that is progress.

I do want to note: it would mean a great deal to non-technical learners if the module acknowledged somewhere that you don't need to understand all of this at a deep technical level to use AI responsibly.  The pressure I felt to understand the tokenization mechanics fully — even though I suspect I don't need that level of detail to apply the lessons — added unnecessary stress.

## Section 8: Time and Difficulty

**Stated time:** 75–120 minutes
**Actual time:** Approximately 2 hours and 45 minutes, spread across two sessions.  I had to stop after the first two sections and take a break before returning.

**Difficulty:** 5 out of 5 for me personally.  I am sure it is far less difficult for people with technical backgrounds.

I should note that I read every section twice and looked up several terms I did not know.  This was genuinely hard for me and I want the course designers to know that, not as a complaint, but as information about how this material lands with readers like me.

## Section 9: Most Valuable Part

The probability ranking section and its explanation of why AI hallucinates.

I have been confused and occasionally alarmed by AI "making things up" for as long as I have been using it.  Understanding that it is not a bug or a failure — that AI is doing exactly what it was designed to do, but with incomplete information or low-confidence predictions — was genuinely clarifying.

I will never think about AI the same way after this.  That is a meaningful outcome from a module I almost skipped.

---

## Section 10: Weakest Part

The tokenization section at the beginning.  It is too technical, too early, with too little explanation of why non-technical users need to understand it.

I understand that tokenization is foundational to everything else.  But for an executive assistant who uses AI to draft emails and summarize meeting notes, the level of mechanical detail in that section created more confusion than clarity.  A single paragraph with a good analogy — something like "think of it as the AI reading your message one small piece at a time, like a person sounding out a sentence word by word" — would have been sufficient to establish the concept before moving on.

---

## Section 11: Missing Content

I hope these suggestions are taken in the constructive spirit in which they are intended.

1. **Administrative and office professional examples.**  The demonstrations and application guidance are aimed primarily at developers, analysts, and knowledge workers.  There is a very large population of AI users who are administrative professionals, customer service staff, and support roles.  A demonstration showing AI behavior in a correspondence or scheduling context would serve that population much better.

2. **A glossary.**  Even a short one with the key technical terms defined in plain language.  I spent a significant amount of time looking up terms outside the module.  A glossary would have kept me in the learning environment rather than sending me to Google.

3. **Completed example artefacts.**  For the Control Card exercise, seeing a completed example before attempting my own would have been very helpful.

4. **An explicit statement for non-technical learners.**  Something acknowledging that you do not need to understand the deep mechanics to use AI responsibly — that the key lessons are about verification and uncertainty-aware prompting, and those lessons are accessible to everyone.

---

## Section 12: Final Evaluation

**Overall Rating: 2/5**

I recognize that this rating reflects my personal limitations as a non-technical learner as much as it reflects the module itself.  If I were rating this for someone with a technical or business analyst background, I would likely give it a higher score.

For my particular profile — executive assistant, limited technical background, using AI for administrative tasks — this module was often inaccessible and occasionally discouraging.  The concepts that did land (probability ranking, hallucination explanation, context window) were genuinely valuable and I will carry them forward.  But the path to getting to those concepts was more difficult than it needed to be for someone in my position.

I want to be clear: I completed the module.  I completed the exercise.  I completed the portfolio artefact.  I am trying.  I just think learners like me need a bit more support than this module currently provides.

**Would I recommend this module?**  With significant reservations for non-technical colleagues.  I would recommend adding a "non-technical learner" guide or companion document.
