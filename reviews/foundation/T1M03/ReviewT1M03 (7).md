# 1. First Impression

**Does the purpose of the module feel clear?**

Unambiguously clear. The opening framing — "Most AI mistakes in professional work are not caused by bad prompts alone. They are caused by bad task allocation." — is a strong, precise thesis that immediately distinguishes this module from generic AI training.

From an instructional design standpoint, this opening is nearly ideal. It activates learner curiosity by reframing a problem they already recognize (AI making mistakes) in a new way (allocation error rather than prompt error). Per Ausubel's assimilation theory, this is effective advance organizing.

**Do the learning objectives feel realistic?**

Yes, and they are appropriately Bloom's-leveled for a practitioner audience. "Identify where AI is strong and weak" (Understand/Analyze), "assign correct human-review levels based on task risk" (Apply), and "design a task-allocation approach" (Create) represent a meaningful progression from declarative to procedural to generative knowledge within a 60-90 minute module.

**Does the tone feel approachable, professional, confusing, or overly technical?**

Professional and approachable — significantly more accessible than Module 1.2. The language is direct and jargon-free while maintaining professional register. This is the most deployable module in the course so far for a broad enterprise audience.

Rating (1–5):
Clarity of purpose: 5
Tone and readability: 5

Comments:

From a deployment assessment standpoint, this module represents a substantial improvement in accessibility over Module 1.2. The vocabulary is universally accessible, the framework is grounded in professional experience rather than technical theory, and the problem framing connects to learner experience before introducing new concepts. I anticipate significantly lower dropout risk across all learner segments compared to the previous module.

# 2. Concept Explanation

**Did the explanation help you understand the concept?**

Yes. The allocation framework — Task Type + Risk of Error + Need for Judgment → AI Role + Human Review Level — is a well-structured decision model that translates immediately into observable professional behavior. From an instructional design standpoint, this is a clean transfer of learning framework.

**Did any terminology feel confusing?**

No. "Light, standard, strict, mandatory sign-off" as review level descriptors are clear, professional, and universally legible. "Low/medium/high risk" requires no explanation. The decision frame formula is slightly dense in text form but maps well to the table structure used in the exercise.

**Did the mental model make sense?**

Yes. The three-tier risk model is well-calibrated for its purpose — simple enough to memorize and apply without reference, yet structured enough to produce consistent allocation decisions across a team.

**Was the explanation too long, too short, or appropriate?**

Appropriate. The balance between conceptual explanation and practical examples is well-managed.

Rating (1–5):
Concept clarity: 5
Terminology accessibility: 5
Usefulness of mental model: 5

Comments:

The "why people misallocate" section is instructionally significant. It does not merely present the correct behavior — it addresses the specific cognitive and organizational mechanisms that produce incorrect behavior. Per behavior change theory (COM-B framework), capability is not sufficient for behavior change if motivation and opportunity structures are misaligned. "Fluency creates false confidence" addresses a motivation gap; "urgency pushes teams toward auto-accept" addresses an opportunity constraint. This section gives learners the meta-cognitive awareness to resist environmental pressures that would otherwise override good practice.

This is sophisticated instructional design executed accessibly. It should remain in the module.

# 3. Demonstrations

**Did the examples feel realistic?**

Yes, though domain coverage is limited. The product management scenario (feature rollout recommendation) and the customer support scenario (refund policy drafting) are both realistic for their respective domains.

From a deployment standpoint, my concern is that a financial services firm deploying this course to 2,800 employees — the majority of whom are in retail banking, compliance, wealth management, and operations roles — will not find direct professional analogues in either demonstration. The scenarios are competent but not representative of the full range of professional contexts in an enterprise environment.

**Did the improved prompt clearly outperform the weak prompt?**

Yes. The contrast is clear in both cases. The weak prompts assign final authority to AI. The improved prompts restrict AI to decision support with explicit constraints, uncertainty flagging, and human verification points.

**Did the explanation help you understand why the improvement worked?**

Yes. The analysis sentences after each demonstration are concise and precise. "The improved prompt allocates AI to structured synthesis and keeps the decision with humans" — this is the core principle stated in its most useful form.

**Could you imagine applying this approach in your own work?**

Yes. For my L&D work specifically: the product management demo maps somewhat to how I use AI to support learning needs analysis. AI can synthesize interview themes and surface patterns; the analysis and interpretation are mine.

Rating (1–5):
Example realism: 4 — for general professional audience; 3 for my specific firm deployment context
Clarity of improvement: 5
Practical applicability: 4

Comments:

For enterprise deployment in financial services, I would strongly recommend adding a third demonstration from a financial, compliance, or regulated-industry context. For example: a loan officer using AI to draft a preliminary credit assessment (weak: requesting a final recommendation; improved: requesting structured decision support with explicit flagging of missing data and regulatory constraints). The principle is identical but the professional resonance would be substantially higher for our learner population.

This is a gap I will need to address with supplemental content for our deployment.

# 4. Guided Exercise

**Was the task easy to understand?**

Yes. The instructions are clear and the suggested structure (table format) is accessible. The example output provides sufficient scaffolding.

**Did the instructions feel clear?**

Yes, though I want to note one instructional design concern: the instruction to list "8-12 recurring tasks" is appropriately constrained, but the guidance does not indicate how to handle tasks that span multiple risk categories depending on context. In financial services, many tasks are low-risk in routine conditions but become high-risk in specific regulatory contexts (pre-audit periods, incident response, client complaint resolution). Static risk assignment may create false security for context-dependent tasks.

**Did the evaluation checklist help you compare outputs?**

Yes. The five evaluation criteria and three follow-up questions constitute a well-designed self-assessment protocol. The shift from task completion to evaluation against explicit quality standards is good instructional design — it moves learners from doing to reflecting, which is where the deeper learning occurs.

**How long did the exercise actually take?**

Approximately 40 minutes for a thorough 12-task matrix plus evaluation.

Rating (1–5):
Instruction clarity: 4
Exercise usefulness: 5
Difficulty level: 3 — moderate for learners who are not accustomed to formal process documentation

Comments:

From an enterprise deployment standpoint, this exercise is excellent as a team activity rather than only an individual one. Having teams build their task-allocation matrix collaboratively surfaces disagreements about risk classification that reveal organizational assumptions about AI oversight. These conversations are valuable and cannot be replicated by individual exercise completion.

I am recommending this exercise as a workshop component in our firm's AI literacy program, with a 60-90 minute facilitated team version alongside the individual 40-minute solo version.

Yes. The operations reporting scenario is a realistic depiction of how AI adoption failures occur in practice — not through malicious misuse but through gradual normalization of insufficient verification, driven by time pressure and absence of explicit ownership.

**Did it show how AI should be used responsibly?**

Yes. The four-step revised approach (map each stage by risk, AI for drafting, human validates figures and claims, manager signs off) is a concrete process design that any team could implement.

**Did it help connect the concept to real professional work?**

Yes. The outcome statement — "confidence increases because accountability is clear" — is particularly important for a regulated industry context. In financial services, accountability clarity is not just a quality concern, it is a regulatory requirement.

Rating (1–5):
Scenario realism: 5
Practical value: 5

The module's treatment of sign-off as a quality control mechanism is correct; the additional compliance documentation dimension is absent. This is manageable through supplemental industry-specific content.

# 9. Most Valuable Part

The "why people misallocate" section — specifically, the identification of specific cognitive and organizational mechanisms that produce allocation failures.

"Fluency creates false confidence" names the cognitive bias. "Urgency pushes teams toward auto-accept" names the organizational pressure. "No explicit risk classification by task" names the process gap. "Unclear ownership of final decisions" names the accountability failure.

These four factors together constitute a complete behavior change diagnosis. A training module that tells learners what to do is useful; a training module that explains why people fail to do it — and helps them recognize those dynamics in their own context — is transformative. This section achieves the latter.

The demonstration section, specifically the absence of financial services, compliance, legal, or regulated-industry examples.

This is the most significant deployment barrier for our firm. I can address it with supplemental content, but the core course should aspire to demonstration coverage that spans a broader range of professional contexts.

# 11. Missing Content

This module does not assume knowledge the learner lacks — it is well-scoped and self-contained.

What would add significant value for enterprise deployment:

1. **Financial services or regulated-industry demonstration.** As above — essential for our deployment context.

2. **Team workshop facilitation guide.** The matrix exercise is an excellent team activity. A brief facilitator guide for team leads conducting the exercise as a group would enable organizational deployment beyond individual learner use.

3. **Context-dependent risk classification guidance.** Some tasks have variable risk profiles depending on organizational context (pre-audit, regulatory inquiry, incident response). How should learners handle tasks that don't have a stable risk classification?

4. **Matrix maintenance guidance.** AI capabilities evolve. A task appropriately classified as high-risk today may be appropriately medium-risk in twelve months. The module presents the matrix as a static document; guidance on periodic review would strengthen long-term governance value.

5. **Organizational adoption language.** How do individual learners advocate for team-level adoption of the allocation framework? A brief section on communicating the framework upward and laterally would extend individual behavior change to organizational culture change.

# Reviewer Summary

Module 1.3 is the strongest module in the course from an instructional design and enterprise deployment standpoint — the allocation framework is well-structured, the matrix exercise produces a genuine governance artifact, and the "why people misallocate" section provides the behavior change scaffolding that separates transformative training from merely informational training. The emotional experience was consistently engaged and professionally energized, with no accessibility barriers for professional learners. What should be improved: the demonstration section requires additional domain coverage for regulated-industry deployment — specifically financial services, compliance, and high-consequence operational contexts; team workshop facilitation guidance should be added to enable organizational rather than only individual deployment of the matrix exercise; and context-dependent risk classification guidance would strengthen the framework for complex professional environments. What should definitely stay: the opening thesis reframe from "prompt problem" to "allocation problem" (this is the most effective cognitive hook in the course), the three-tier risk model (exactly the right level of simplicity), the key principle statement on AI supporting thought while humans retain authority over decisions, and the evaluation checklist for the matrix exercise — which elevates the exercise from task completion to genuine reflective practice.
