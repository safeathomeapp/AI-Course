# 2. Concept Explanation

**Did the explanation help you understand the concept?**

Yes. The allocation framework — Task Type + Risk of Error + Need for Judgment → AI Role + Human Review Level — maps directly to how I already think about work distribution in operations. This is resource allocation with a different kind of resource.

**Did any terminology feel confusing?**

No. "Light, standard, strict, mandatory sign-off" as review level descriptors are immediately legible. "Low/medium/high risk" is standard operational risk language. No barriers.

**Did the mental model make sense?**

Yes. The three-tier risk model is clean and deployable. I ran through my team's recurring tasks mentally while reading and the framework held up — KPI reporting feels like medium risk, vendor compliance documentation feels like high risk, shift handoff notes feel like low risk.

**Was the explanation too long, too short, or appropriate?**

Appropriate. I appreciated the directness. No padding.

The section on "why people misallocate" is important and correct. "Urgency pushes teams toward auto-accept" — yes. In fast-paced logistics operations, there is constant pressure to move quickly. If AI produces a plausible-looking output, teams will use it without adequate review if there is no explicit protocol mandating otherwise. You cannot solve this with training alone — you need process controls. This module understands that.

Rating (1–5):
Concept clarity: 5
Terminology accessibility: 5
Usefulness of mental model: 8/10

Comments:

The key principle — "AI should support thought, speed, and structure; humans should retain authority over correctness, risk, and final decisions" — is the governance principle I have been trying to articulate. Clear enough to put in a policy document. I will be using this language.

One gap: the framework treats "high risk" as a single category. In my operations environment, there are meaningful distinctions within high risk — a documentation error in a safety report has different consequences than an error in a vendor performance assessment. The model is a useful starting point but practitioners will need guidance on how to calibrate within tiers.

# 9. Most Valuable Part

The "why people misallocate" section — specifically, "urgency pushes teams toward auto-accept."

This is the operational reality that most AI guidance ignores. In fast-paced professional environments, teams will not implement careful verification protocols spontaneously. Urgency will override process discipline. The solution is not asking people to slow down — it is building explicit protocols that define what level of human review is mandatory before an AI output can be acted on, regardless of time pressure.

The module names this dynamic accurately. The task-risk allocation matrix is the mechanism for converting that insight into durable process design.

---

# 10. Weakest Part

The demonstrations. Two scenarios from product management and customer support, neither of which directly addresses logistics, manufacturing, operations, or supply chain — despite these being major AI deployment environments with significant stakes.

I understand the course cannot demonstrate every domain. But an operations or logistics example would serve a large professional audience that currently has to translate PM and CS scenarios into their own context without guidance. The cost of an AI error in a safety report is categorically different from the cost of an AI error in a feature recommendation. The demonstrations should reflect that range of consequence.

---

# 11. Missing Content

The module is well-scoped and does not assume knowledge I lack.

What would add substantial value:

1. **Operations, logistics, or manufacturing demonstration.** As above — a high-stakes operational scenario would serve a large professional audience that is currently invisible in the course's example set.

2. **Within-tier calibration guidance.** How do you distinguish between a 3/5 high-risk task and a 5/5 high-risk task? Some high-risk tasks require mandatory sign-off from a department head; others require only senior team member review. The framework needs more granularity for deployment in complex professional environments.

3. **Team implementation guidance.** The module teaches individual task allocation. But AI deployment is a team management challenge. How do you enforce allocation protocols when your team is using AI tools independently? What governance mechanisms are appropriate at a team level?

4. **Guidance for evolving AI capabilities.** The risk profile of a task can change as AI capabilities improve. A task that warranted strict review six months ago might now be reliably handled with standard review. The matrix is presented as a fixed document; guidance on periodic review and revision would strengthen it.
