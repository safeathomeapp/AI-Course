# 2. Concept Explanation

**Did the explanation help you understand the concept?**

Yes. The decision frame — Task Type + Risk of Error + Need for Judgment → AI Role + Human Review Level — formalizes an implicit process I've been running for 18 months. Seeing it written out as a structured model makes it auditable rather than just intuitive.

**Did any terminology feel confusing?**

No. "Light, standard, strict, mandatory sign-off" is clear professional language that needs no explanation.

**Did the mental model make sense?**

Yes. The three-tier risk model maps directly to how I already think about my work, even if I haven't been applying it consistently:
- Low risk: research summaries, brainstorms, draft social copy — fast AI, quick check
- Medium risk: client strategy memos, campaign analysis — AI draft, structured review before sending
- High risk: competitor intelligence with named claims, client-facing analysis with data sourcing — strict verification before it leaves my hands

**Was the explanation too long, too short, or appropriate?**

Appropriate. Could have been slightly shorter on the AI-is-strong/weak lists — those are somewhat obvious. The decision frame itself is the valuable part.

Rating (1–5):
Concept clarity: 5
Terminology accessibility: 5
Usefulness of mental model: 9/10

Comments:

"Fluency creates false confidence" — yes. This is the mechanism behind every near-miss I've had. AI writes confident marketing copy with a fabricated statistic and I almost send it to a client because it reads well. The stat isn't supported but nothing in the prose signals that. The verification failure happens because the output quality masks the underlying accuracy gap.

"Urgency pushes teams toward auto-accept" — for a solo operator working against tight client deadlines, this is the dominant risk factor. I don't have a team to override time pressure. I'm my own worst enemy when a deliverable is due in two hours.

# 4. Guided Exercise

**Was the task easy to understand?**

Yes. Build a task-allocation matrix for 8-12 recurring tasks. Clear instructions, useful scaffolding.

**Did the instructions feel clear?**

Yes. The evaluation checklist is well-designed — five criteria plus three follow-up questions is the right scope for a 20-30 minute exercise.

**How long did the exercise actually take?**

About 25 minutes. Fast for me — I know my workflow well.

Instruction clarity: 4

# 10. Weakest Part

The demonstrations. Both scenarios are from domains that aren't directly relevant to marketing, consulting, or agency work.

This is a concrete gap, not a stylistic preference. The specific failure modes when AI is misallocated in marketing work — fabricated statistics in competitive analysis, invented market share figures in positioning documents, AI-generated client quotes in case studies — are different from the failure modes in product management or customer support. Without a marketing example, I had to construct the parallels myself, which I can do but many practitioners won't.

# 11. Missing Content

The module is self-contained. No missing prerequisite knowledge.

What would add value for my context:

1. **Marketing, consulting, or agency demonstration.** As above.

2. **ROI framing for solo operators.** The allocation framework requires time investment (building a matrix, adding verification steps). For a solo operator, every process step has a time cost. The module would benefit from acknowledging the efficiency trade-off and helping practitioners decide when the verification investment is worth the time cost.

3. **Guidance on client disclosure.** When does a consultant need to disclose AI involvement in deliverables? This is a business and professional ethics question that sits adjacent to the allocation framework and is completely absent.

4. **Competitive intelligence specifics.** AI-assisted competitor research carries specific fabrication risks (invented statistics, market share claims, attributed positions) that deserve explicit treatment. This is one of the highest-risk AI use cases in marketing and is not addressed.
