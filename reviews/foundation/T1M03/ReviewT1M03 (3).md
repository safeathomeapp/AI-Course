Rating (1–5):

# 3. Demonstrations

**Did the examples feel realistic?**

The customer support demonstration (refund policy) felt very realistic to me.  I work closely with our customer-facing teams and I understand immediately why asking AI to "write a final refund policy we can use with customers" is problematic.  The improved version, which asks AI to draft an internal process note for review by the policy owner, makes complete sense.

The product management demonstration was somewhat less familiar to me.  I understand project management but "feature rollout" is not a scenario I encounter directly.  I was able to follow the logic, but I felt more engaged with the customer support example.

**Did the improved prompt clearly outperform the weak prompt?**

Yes, in both cases.  The difference is immediately apparent.  The weak examples ask AI to make final decisions.  The improved examples ask AI to support human decision-making.  That distinction is now very clear to me.

**Did the explanation help you understand why the improvement worked?**

Yes.  The analysis paragraphs after each demonstration ("The improved prompt allocates AI to structured synthesis and keeps the decision with humans" and "The weak prompt gives AI authority it should not have") were concise and helpful.

**Could you imagine applying this approach in your own work?**

Yes.  I think about how I use Copilot to draft correspondence for my executive.  I have sometimes asked it to write a final letter based on very limited information.  Under this module's framework, that should be a "draft for review" with explicit acknowledgment that I am providing partial context — not a request for a finished product.

Rating (1–5):
Example realism: 4

Practical applicability: 4

# 9. Most Valuable Part

The risk-tier model — Low risk: AI draft + quick human scan / Medium risk: AI draft + structured human verification / High risk: AI support only + strict human review + documented sign-off.

This is simple enough to memorize, clear enough to apply immediately, and consequential enough to change behavior.  I will use this model every time I decide whether to use Copilot for a task.

Before this module, my mental model was essentially: "Does this task seem important enough to double-check?"  That is a much weaker standard.  The three-tier model gives me a structured approach that I can apply consistently rather than relying on subjective judgment about importance.

# 10. Weakest Part

The product management demonstration.  It is a good example in principle, but it is abstract for learners who are not in product or strategy roles.  I followed the logic but I did not feel the personal relevance I felt with the customer support example.

I would suggest replacing or supplementing the product management demonstration with an example from a role that more of the course's learners occupy — administrative support, customer-facing work, financial operations, or similar.  The demonstration section should feel directly relevant to learners across different professional backgrounds, not only those in knowledge-worker or product roles.

# 11. Missing Content

This module did not assume knowledge I did not have — which is a genuine improvement from Module 1.2.

However, I would find the following additions helpful:

1. **An administrative or support role demonstration.**  A before/after example involving correspondence management, briefing preparation, or executive support would make the demonstrations far more applicable to EA, administrative, and support professionals.

2. **Guidance on communicating AI use practices to supervisors.**  I now have a matrix and a framework.  How do I bring this to my executive and explain that I have implemented responsible AI practices?  A brief section on this professional communication dimension would be useful for employees at all levels.

3. **A note on what to do when you are unsure of the risk level.**  The three tiers are clear for extreme cases (quick email = low risk, legal commitment = high risk) but some tasks genuinely fall in uncertain territory.  Is there a default rule when you cannot decide?
