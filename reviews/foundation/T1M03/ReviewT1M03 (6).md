# 1. First Impression

**Does the purpose of the module feel clear?**

Yes, and immediately so. "Most AI mistakes in professional work are not caused by bad prompts alone. They are caused by bad task allocation." I stopped reading for a moment when I got to that sentence.

I have been thinking about AI problems as craft problems — better writing prompts, clearer briefs, more specific instructions. This module suggests the prior question is whether certain tasks should be AI-assisted at all, and at what level of human oversight. That reframe is meaningful.

**Do the learning objectives feel realistic?**

Yes. Identify AI strengths and weaknesses, assign review levels, design a task-allocation approach — achievable within the module's scope and immediately applicable.

**Does the tone feel approachable, professional, confusing, or overly technical?**

Approachable and professional. The most readable module in the course so far, and probably the most broadly accessible. No technical vocabulary barriers.

Rating (1–5):
Clarity of purpose: 5
Tone and readability: 5

Comments:

There's something right about the directness here. The module doesn't hedge. "AI should support thought, speed, and structure. Humans should retain authority over correctness, risk, and final decisions." That's a clear professional principle, stated without caveat or false balance. For someone who has been trying to figure out where AI fits in editorial work, having a framework stated this clearly is useful.

# 2. Concept Explanation

**Did the explanation help you understand the concept?**

Yes. The decision frame — Task Type + Risk of Error + Need for Judgment → AI Role + Human Review Level — gave me a way to think about AI's role in content work that I hadn't had before. The distinction between "AI for first-draft writing and option generation" (appropriate) and "AI for final editorial voice, accountability for claims, policy ownership" (not appropriate) is exactly where I've been trying to draw a line.

**Did any terminology feel confusing?**

No. The module's vocabulary is unusually accessible. "Light, standard, strict, mandatory sign-off" as review levels are clear without explanation.

**Did the mental model make sense?**

Yes. The three-tier risk model maps well to editorial work in a way I found clarifying:
- Low risk: initial brainstorm, structural outline, research summary for my own use
- Medium risk: first draft of client content for internal review
- High risk: final published content with attributed claims, branded voice material, compliance-sensitive communications

That mapping mostly held up as I worked through it.

**Was the explanation too long, too short, or appropriate?**

Appropriate. Possibly the first module where I wanted more rather than less — specifically more on the medium-risk tier, which is where most of my professional work lives and which gets less treatment than the clear extremes.

Rating (1–5):
Concept clarity: 5
Terminology accessibility: 5
Usefulness of mental model: 8/10

Comments:

The "fluency creates false confidence" principle resonates deeply in a content context. AI writing is fluent in a way that obscures the distinction between "written smoothly" and "written accurately." In editorial work, those are different standards and conflating them is a professional hazard. Having a named concept for the failure mode is useful — I can now articulate to clients why AI-generated content requires human editorial review in a way that doesn't sound like defensiveness about my role.

I'm conflicted about one thing: the framework positions "first-draft writing" clearly in the AI-appropriate column. That's operationally useful guidance. But it also carries assumptions about what "first-draft writing" means in a content strategy context that may not hold in all cases. A first draft for a thought leadership piece attributed to a CEO carries different stakes than a first draft for an internal FAQ. The framework needs a "what kind of first draft?" layer that it doesn't currently have.

# 11. Missing Content

The module is self-contained and doesn't assume knowledge I lacked.

Suggested additions:

1. **Content and editorial demonstration.** As described above — the AI-in-content-creation space is underserved by this module's demonstrations.

2. **Medium-risk tier guidance.** The framework is clearest at the extremes (note summarization = obviously low; final legal wording = obviously high). Most professional AI work lives in the medium tier and the module gives it less treatment than it deserves. What are the specific verification practices appropriate for medium-risk content tasks?

3. **"What kind of first draft" distinction.** "First-draft writing" appears straightforwardly in the AI-appropriate column. For content professionals, this needs qualification — a first draft for an internal process document is different from a first draft for a published article under a named author's byline. Risk level varies dramatically within the "first-draft writing" category.

4. **Disclosure guidance.** When should professionals disclose AI involvement to clients or audiences? This is a governance question that belongs adjacent to the allocation framework and is absent from the module.
