I wrote this formula in my notebook. In product management, I make similar analysis for feature prioritization — high impact, low effort = prioritize; high risk = extra review. Same thinking structure.

**Did any terminology feel confusing?**

No. "Low risk, medium risk, high risk" are universal terms. "Light, standard, strict, mandatory sign-off" are easy to understand. No dictionary needed.

**Did the mental model make sense?**

Yes. Three-tier model is very clear. I immediately thought about my own tasks:
- Writing product requirements first draft = low to medium risk, AI can help
- Final approval communication to stakeholders = high risk, human must decide
- Meeting summary = low risk, AI draft is fine

Framework worked well for my professional context.

**Was the explanation too long, too short, or appropriate?**

Appropriate length. Not too long. Each section says what is needed and stops.

Rating (1–5):
Concept clarity: 5
Terminology accessibility: 5
Usefulness of mental model: 5

Comments:

"Fluency creates false confidence" — this sentence is important. In Japanese, we have word 雰囲気読み (fun-iki yomi) — reading the atmosphere, accepting something because it feels right without checking carefully. AI output often looks professional and confident. # 10. Weakest Part

The demonstrations do not include product development, technical product management, or software company context.

Product management demonstration covers feature decision support — this is relevant. But there is no example involving technical documentation, engineering communication, or cross-functional product coordination. These are common AI use cases in technology companies that are not represented.

Also: both demonstrations are from Western organizational contexts. In Japanese companies, decision-making process often involves nemawashi (pre-meeting consensus building) and ringi (formal approval documentation). AI integration in these processes may have different characteristics than in the demonstrations. Course could benefit from acknowledgment that organizational culture affects allocation decisions.

# 11. Missing Content

Module is accessible and did not assume knowledge I lacked.

Suggested additions:

1. **Technical product or engineering communication scenario.** Common use case for product managers, technical writers, and developer-adjacent roles.

2. **Cultural context note.** In some organizational cultures, urgency is not the primary risk factor — social pressure to accept AI output without questioning, or unclear responsibility in consensus-based decision structures, may be more significant. Brief acknowledgment that cultural context affects misallocation risk would be useful for international learners.

3. **More complete example matrix.** Current example has two rows. Full example matrix with 8-10 rows would better demonstrate how variety of task types should be classified together.

4. **Guidance on collaborative matrix building.** In team settings, having multiple people classify same tasks may produce different risk assessments. Brief guidance on how to resolve these disagreements would be useful.
