# Module 1.7 AI as a Thinking Partner

## Module Overview

**Tier:** Foundations  
**Estimated Duration:** 60-90 minutes  
**Prerequisites:** Module 1.3 and Module 1.6

### Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, learners will be able to:

- use AI for critique, challenge, and alternative framing
- run simple critique loops to improve decisions
- prevent confirmation bias when using AI support

---

## 1. Concept Explanation

AI can be used as a thinking partner by asking it to challenge your assumptions, identify risks, and propose alternatives.

Why this matters:
- many decisions fail from untested assumptions
- critique loops improve quality before execution
- better questions produce better analysis

How AI behavior relates to this concept:
- AI can generate multiple viewpoints quickly
- AI may mirror your bias if prompts are one-sided
- explicit challenge instructions improve critical output

Where learners commonly go wrong:
- using AI only to confirm their existing view
- asking for "pros only" and ignoring risks
- skipping final human synthesis

Short example: A marketing plan improves when AI is asked to argue why the plan might fail before launch.

---

## 2. Demonstration

### Example Context
Marketing and content: evaluate a campaign concept.

### Weak Example
"Give feedback on this campaign idea."

### Improved Example
"You are a critical strategy reviewer.

Context:
I have drafted a campaign idea targeting first-time buyers.

Task:
Stress-test the concept and identify likely failure points.

Constraints:
- Prioritize substance over wording
- Identify assumptions and missing evidence
- Do not assume the concept is correct

Output Format:
1. Main weaknesses
2. Missing assumptions
3. Risks
4. Improvements
5. Questions I must answer before launch"

### Analysis
The improved prompt creates adversarial analysis instead of soft feedback. It uses AI to challenge thinking, not to validate bias.

## 3. Guided Exercise

### Task
Run a three-step critique loop on one work idea.

### Instructions
1. Ask AI to summarize your idea neutrally.
2. Ask AI to critique assumptions and risks.
3. Ask AI to propose a revised version with stronger controls.

### Suggested Structure
- Initial idea:
- Step 1 summary:
- Step 2 critique:
- Step 3 revision:
- Human decision:

### Example Output
Summary: New onboarding email sequence to improve activation.  
Critique: No segment-specific messaging; unclear success metric.  
Revision: Add two user segments, define 14-day activation KPI, include rollback criteria.

---

## 4. Real-World Application

Thinking-partner use cases include:
- product: challenge feature assumptions before roadmap commitment
- finance: test risk scenarios before recommendations
- software: evaluate trade-offs before architecture changes
- operations: stress-test process updates before rollout
- support: assess policy changes from customer and agent perspectives

---

## 5. Common Mistakes

- asking only for agreement
- not requiring counterarguments
- ignoring uncertain or weak evidence
- using AI critique without human synthesis

These mistakes create false confidence and weak decisions.

---

## 6. Portfolio Artefact

### Artefact Title
Decision Critique Log

### What the Learner Produces
A log of 3 decisions with:
- original idea
- AI critique output
- revised decision and rationale

### Why It Matters
This demonstrates analytical maturity and practical AI-assisted reasoning.

---

## 7. Reflection Questions

- Do you use AI more for confirmation or challenge?
- Which critique question improved your thinking most?
- Where should human judgment override AI suggestions?
