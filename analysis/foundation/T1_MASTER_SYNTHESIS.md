# T1 Master Synthesis

## Scope
Foundation Tier aggregation across first four reviewed modules, using existing module-level analysis outputs only.

## Modules Reviewed
- T1M01
- T1M02
- T1M03
- T1M04

## Repeated Strengths Across T1M01-T1M04
1. Core philosophy is stable and repeatedly validated: AI assists, human review and decision authority remain central.
2. Mental-model teaching works: each module's central framework (6-step model, LLM reliability model, task-risk allocation, RCTCO) is retained and applied by learners.
3. Practical exercises consistently generate behavior change when learners complete them with real work inputs.
4. Portfolio artefacts are repeatedly viewed as professionally useful, not just course-completion outputs.
5. Clarity/readability trend improves through the sequence, with T1M03-T1M04 especially strong on accessibility and applicability.
6. Cross-module continuity is increasingly coherent (T1M03 governance layer connects well to T1M04 prompting control layer).

## Repeated Concerns Across T1M01-T1M04
1. Role-relevance coverage remains uneven; learners in several personas repeatedly report translation burden from examples.
2. Terminology clarity/consistency remains a recurring friction point, especially in early modules and for non-technical cohorts.
3. Practice calibration is inconsistent: learners repeatedly request stronger anchors for scoring, worked examples, and artefact-quality expectations.
4. Beginner onboarding friction persists in early foundation modules (technical load, tool readiness, confidence drop-off).
5. Team-scale deployment guidance is weaker than individual-use guidance in modules where artefacts naturally become team resources.
6. Regulated-context caveats recur as low-frequency but high-severity risk signals.

## Strongest Confirmed Issues
1. Foundation-wide role relevance/example transfer gap (`FND-001`).
2. Practice scaffolding and calibration gap across exercises/artefacts (`FND-003`).
3. Beginner-entry friction in early foundation sequence (`FND-004`).
4. Terminology clarity/consistency gap across multiple modules (`FND-002`).
5. Team operationalization gap in later foundation modules (`FND-006`).

## Mixed / Conditional Issues
1. Regulated-context governance signal (`FND-005`) is low-frequency but high-impact; should be treated as a conditional safety layer, not ignored as noise.
2. Accessibility friction (`FND-007`) is highly cohort-dependent and improves when structure/language quality improves.
3. Checklist/rubric anchor concerns are strongest in educator/manager deployment contexts and lighter for self-study users.
4. Advanced-depth and deployment-packaging asks (`FND-012`) are real demand signals but mostly outside core foundation pedagogical scope.

## Persona-Specific Friction
1. Beginners/non-technical learners: early technical density, unclear entry path, and tool-readiness assumptions reduce confidence and increase completion time.
2. Developers/heavy users: ask for stronger domain fit and optional depth; less blocked by core concepts, more sensitive to perceived relevance.
3. Teachers/trainers/L&D evaluators: need scoring anchors, consistency mechanisms, and team deployment guidance for scale.
4. Managers/enterprise evaluators: repeatedly seek governance, approval, maintenance, and rollout patterns beyond individual learner usage.
5. Regulated/compliance personas: require explicit safe-use caveats and documentation implications for AI-assisted work.
6. International/non-native and neurodivergent learners: benefit strongly from structured formatting, plain language, and explicit templates; friction rises with jargon/density.

## Stability Signals
1. Do not change course-level accountability stance (human decision ownership).
2. Do not remove framework-centric teaching; this is the strongest retention mechanism across modules.
3. Do not remove real-work comparative exercises; they are repeatedly the highest-impact learning activity.
4. Do not remove artefact outputs; they are repeatedly the strongest transfer mechanism.
5. Preserve readability gains achieved in T1M03-T1M04.

## Early Revision Implications
1. Highest leverage is horizontal: shared fixes (example coverage strategy, terminology discipline, exercise calibration standards) can improve multiple modules at once.
2. Foundation sequence should use a beginner-safe entry standard in early modules without flattening advanced value.
3. Add optional overlays rather than bloating core modules: regulated-context caveats, team deployment notes, and advanced depth paths can be lightweight and modular.
4. Module-specific improvements remain necessary where single-module barriers are dominant (T1M02 density, T1M03 medium-risk calibration, T1M04 constraints method).
