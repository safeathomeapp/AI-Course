Module 1 · Foundations

AI is a tool for generating drafts, not a substitute for judgement.

This module introduces the practical mental model for using AI in real work: a human starts the task, provides context, the AI generates a draft, and the human reviews and decides what happens next.

🧭 Key principle

AI drafts. Humans review. Humans decide.

Use AI to accelerate first-pass work, but keep responsibility, checking, and final decisions with a person.

⚠ Important boundary

Fast output is not the same as reliable output.

AI can sound confident while still being incomplete, unsuitable, or wrong. That is why review is a required step, not an optional extra.

Core concept

What AI is, in practical terms

🤖 What AI does well

It generates drafts and options quickly

AI is good at producing summaries, rewrites, outlines, first drafts, alternative phrasings, and structured outputs when given enough context.

🧑 What humans still own

Accuracy, judgement, risk, and final decisions

A person remains responsible for checking whether an output is correct, suitable, complete, safe, and appropriate for the real situation.

Plain-language definition

AI is a tool that can generate useful outputs from prompts and context, but it does not replace professional responsibility.

Operating model

The human and AI workflow

The sequence matters. The human initiates the task and sets direction. The AI assists with generation. The human then reviews and decides.

🧑 Human

Sets the task

Defines what needs doing, why it matters, and what constraints apply.

🤖 AI

Produces a draft

🧑 Human

Checks and decides

Tests the output against reality, standards, purpose, and risk.

Concept to example

Weak prompt vs improved prompt

Better output usually starts with a better input. A vague request invites a vague result.

Weak prompt

Too little structure

Write me a professional email.

This leaves the tool guessing about audience, purpose, tone, length, and constraints.

Improved prompt

Clearer task and context

You are helping me draft a short professional email to a supplier.
Purpose: request a corrected invoice.
Tone: polite and direct.
Constraints: under 120 words, plain English, include a request for confirmation by Friday.

This is far more likely to produce a useful first draft because the task is framed properly.

🔍 What changed?

  • The task became specific
  • The audience was implied clearly
  • The tone was defined
  • The output length was constrained
  • A measurable success condition was included

Guided practice

Improve the prompt

Rewrite the vague prompt below so that an AI tool has enough context to generate a better first draft.

Exercise brief

Starting prompt: “Summarise this meeting.”

Add the information a useful assistant would need, such as audience, purpose, format, and any output constraints.

✅ Self-check

  • Did you state the task clearly?
  • Did you define who the output is for?
  • Did you specify a format?
  • Did you include any limits or priorities?
  • Would a third party understand what “good” looks like from your prompt?

Workplace relevance

Where this shows up in real work

🤖 Suitable AI tasks

  • Drafting a first version of an email
  • Summarising notes into a clearer format
  • Generating headline options or outlines
  • Rewriting text for a different tone

🧑 Required human checks

  • Is it factually correct?
  • Is anything missing?
  • Is the tone appropriate?
  • Does it create any business, legal, or reputational risk?

🧩 Scenario

Client update draft

A team member needs a quick client update based on rough notes. AI can produce a usable first draft fast, but a person still needs to check whether the facts, promises, dates, and tone are correct before anything is sent.

Key takeaways

🤖

AI generates

It produces candidate outputs based on the prompt and context it receives.

🧑

Humans review

People check for accuracy, omissions, suitability, and practical risk.

🧑

Humans decide

The final responsibility stays with the user, not the tool.
