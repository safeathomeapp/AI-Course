Module header

Introduction to AI as a Tool

In progress

Module 1.1 · 60–90 minutes · No prerequisites

AI is a tool, not an authority.

This module introduces the operating rule for the course: AI drafts. Humans review. Humans decide. The point is not to avoid AI. The point is to use it deliberately, with scope, verification, and final accountability kept by a person.

Core operating rule

Use AI for drafting support, not for automatic trust.

Fluent output can still be unsupported, incomplete, or wrong. Review is a required step, especially when an output will affect decisions or downstream work.

Why beginners get caught out

Fluency is helpful, but fluency is not evidence.

Models can produce convincing language even when the facts are missing, assumptions are hidden, or the prompt is under-specified.

Objective 1

Explain why AI should be treated as a tool, not an authority.

Objective 2

Distinguish useful drafting support from high-risk misuse.

Objective 3

Apply a practical human-review process before acting on output.

Concept explanation

The practical mental model

Use this model whenever you work with AI. It makes the responsibility boundary explicit and stops generated drafts from being mistaken for approved decisions.

1 Prompt
→
2 Context
→
3 Generation
→
4 Human Review
→
5 Decision

What the steps mean

  • Prompt: instructions, goals, constraints, and format.
  • Context: the information that is actually in scope.
  • Generation: a response predicted from learned patterns.
  • Human Review: check facts, assumptions, and fitness for purpose.
  • Decision: a person remains accountable for use.

Key rule

If you skip review, you are delegating authority to a generated draft.

That is the exact behaviour this module is designed to prevent.

Why hallucinations happen

If a prompt is under-specified, or the required facts are not present in context, the model may still produce a smooth answer by filling gaps with likely language. That can look convincing even when it is unsupported.

Why instructions matter

Better instructions do not make AI infallible, but they narrow scope, reduce guessing, and expose uncertainty earlier. That makes outputs easier to review and safer to use.

Capabilities and limits

What AI is strong at, and what it is weak at

Often strong at

  • First-pass drafting
  • Summarizing long materials
  • Extracting patterns from provided text
  • Turning unstructured notes into structured formats
  • Generating alternatives for brainstorming or critique

Often weak at

  • Guaranteed factual correctness without checks
  • High-stakes policy, legal, or compliance final decisions
  • Understanding unstated organisational context
  • Owning accountability for outcomes
  • Safely operating where the cost of error is high

Common beginner failure pattern

  1. User asks a vague question.
  2. AI gives a confident answer.
  3. User assumes correctness.
  4. Output is used without verification.
  5. Error appears later in the workflow.

Better professional pattern

  1. User defines role, context, task, constraints, and format.
  2. AI provides a draft with explicit structure.
  3. User checks facts, assumptions, and fit for audience.
  4. User approves, edits, or rejects.
  5. Decision remains human-owned.

Demonstration

Structured prompts create review-ready output

The demonstration compares a vague prompt with a structured one. The examples below keep the same logic while switching to different work contexts.

Weak prompt

Summarize these notes.

Likely result: a generic narrative summary with hidden unknowns and no clear action structure.

Improved prompt

You are a project coordinator.
Convert these notes into an action log.
Use columns: Action, Owner, Deadline, Status.
Mark unknowns as Unassigned.

Likely result: a usable structure, explicit unknowns, and less downstream rework during review.

Weak prompt

Summarize this meeting.

Likely result: missing owners, missing deadlines, and hidden handoff issues.

Improved prompt

Role: Project coordination assistant
Context: Weekly internal meeting notes covering onboarding delays and handoff issues
Task: Convert notes into a review-ready action brief
Constraints: Use only the notes provided; do not invent commitments; mark unknown owners and deadlines as Unassigned
Output Format: 3-bullet summary, action table, open questions

Likely result: clearer follow-up actions, explicit unknowns, and faster decision readiness.

Weak prompt

Summarize these KPI notes.

Likely result: a smooth summary that may blur confirmed facts, assumptions, and missing figures.

Improved prompt

Role: Finance analyst assistant
Context: KPI notes from the monthly performance review
Task: Turn the notes into a fact / unknown summary for management review
Constraints: Separate confirmed figures from assumptions; do not infer missing numbers; flag review questions explicitly
Output Format: Confirmed facts, Unknowns, Risks, Review questions

Likely result: facts are separated from gaps, and missing information becomes reviewable instead of hidden.

Weak prompt

Write a follow-up email after this meeting.

Likely result: a generic email that may invent ownership or sound more certain than the meeting notes support.

Improved prompt

Role: Administrative assistant
Context: This is a follow-up email after an internal project meeting about onboarding delays
Task: Draft a clear follow-up email with actions and next steps
Constraints: Do not invent owners or deadlines; mark missing details as unknown
Output Format: Subject line, email body, action list, open questions

Likely result: clearer communication, explicit unknowns, and an easier line-by-line review before sending.

Why the improved version is better

  • It narrows scope.
  • It reduces guessing.
  • It makes unknowns visible.
  • It creates structure that is safer to review.

Guided exercise

Turn one vague prompt into a structured prompt

Use the five-part prompt structure from this course: Role, Context, Task, Constraints, and Output Format.

Readiness check

  • You have access to an approved AI tool.
  • You are using non-sensitive practice material.
  • You have 15-20 minutes for one draft, one revision, and one review.

Beginner-safe fallback

Rewrite a vague email-drafting prompt using the five-part structure before running both versions in the same AI tool.

RoleWho the AI is helping as
ContextWhat information is in scope
TaskWhat you want it to do
ConstraintsWhat it must not do
Output FormatHow the result should be shaped

Evaluation checklist

  • Clarity: Is the response easy to understand?
  • Structure: Is the response organised for immediate use?
  • Assumptions: Are assumptions explicit instead of hidden?
  • Missing Information: Does it identify unknowns or gaps?
  • Usefulness for Decision Making: Can a manager act on this safely?

Scoring anchor: 1 = weak or unsafe, 3 = partly usable, 5 = review-ready draft.

Real-world application

Turning rough notes into a decision-support draft

Version A · vague prompt

"Summarize this meeting."

  • Generic narrative summary
  • Missing owners
  • Missing deadlines
  • Hidden unknowns

Version B · structured prompt

Review-ready action brief

  • Role: Project coordination assistant
  • Context: Weekly internal meeting notes covering onboarding delays and handoff issues
  • Task: Convert notes into a review-ready action brief
  • Constraints: Use only the notes provided; do not invent commitments
  • Output Format: 3-bullet summary, action table, open questions

Required human review process

  1. Check that every action is grounded in the original notes.
  2. Confirm owners and deadlines before distribution.
  3. Remove or rewrite anything the AI inferred without evidence.
  4. Approve only after a human reviewer confirms the draft is fit for use.

Portfolio artefact

AI Tool-Use Operating Checklist

Produce a one-page checklist that makes review depth and sign-off explicit before AI output is used in real work.

Checklist fields

  1. Task Type
  2. Risk Level (Low / Medium / High)
  3. AI Role (Drafting / Summarising / Analysis)
  4. Required Human Verification
  5. Final Decision Owner
  6. Unknowns Flagged (Yes / No)
  7. Safe-to-Share Status

Why it matters

This artefact is usable immediately in real work. It demonstrates not just prompting skill, but responsible AI operating discipline.

Reflection and further practice

Close the module by making it concrete

Reflection questions

  • Where in your work are you currently treating AI as an authority?
  • Which outputs in your role require mandatory human review every time?
  • What task this week could be improved by structured prompts plus review checks?

Optional advanced exploration

  • Compare outputs from two AI tools using the same structured prompt.
  • Add risk levels and define review depth for each.
  • Test whether requiring "facts, assumptions, unknowns" reduces false confidence.

Suggested further practice

Pick one recurring weekly task and build a repeatable AI draft-plus-review workflow. Run it once, record what failed, and update your checklist.
