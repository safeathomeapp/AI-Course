# Module 1.3 Understanding AI Strengths and Limitations

## Module Overview

**Tier:** Foundations  
**Estimated Duration:** 60-90 minutes  
**Prerequisites:** Module 1.1 and Module 1.2

### Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, learners will be able to:

- identify tasks where AI provides strong value
- identify tasks where AI output requires deeper human control
- choose an appropriate human-review level by task risk

---

## 1. Concept Explanation

AI is strong at pattern-based language tasks but weaker at guaranteed factual accuracy, nuanced judgment, and accountability decisions.

Why this matters:
- wrong task allocation wastes time
- high-risk decisions require human ownership
- task-risk matching improves both speed and quality

How AI behavior relates to this concept:
- strengths: drafting, summarizing, transforming formats, brainstorming
- limitations: factual precision without sources, legal/compliance certainty, context outside prompt
- output quality changes quickly with prompt quality

Where learners commonly go wrong:
- using AI for final approval tasks
- using AI where source data is incomplete
- skipping risk-based review levels

Short example: AI can draft a product launch brief quickly, but launch decisions still require stakeholder review and real metrics.

---

## 2. Demonstration

### Example Context
Product management: deciding whether to ship a feature.

### Weak Example
"Should we launch this feature next month?"

### Improved Example
"You are a product analyst assistant.

Context:
We have user feedback themes, engineering estimates, and known risks.

Task:
Produce a decision support draft, not a final decision.

Constraints:
- Separate evidence from assumptions
- Include risks and unknowns
- Do not provide authoritative go/no-go conclusion

Output Format:
1. Evidence summary
2. Benefits
3. Risks
4. Unknowns
5. Suggested human decision criteria"

### Analysis
The improved prompt uses AI for structured analysis while preserving human decision accountability. It uses AI as a tool, not an authority.

## 3. Guided Exercise

### Task
Categorize your own work tasks into "AI strong fit" and "human critical."

### Instructions
1. List 8 recurring tasks from your role.
2. Label each task as low, medium, or high risk if wrong.
3. Define whether AI drafts, AI assists, or humans own final output.

### Suggested Structure
| Task | Risk Level | AI Role | Human Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| | | | |

### Example Output
| Task | Risk Level | AI Role | Human Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| Monthly report summary | Medium | Draft summary | Validate numbers and message |
| Legal policy statement | High | Suggest outline | Write and approve final text |

---

## 4. Real-World Application

Task-fit mapping helps in:
- finance: AI drafts commentary, humans validate financial correctness
- software: AI explains code, engineers confirm behavior in codebase
- marketing: AI proposes concepts, humans select strategy
- operations: AI drafts SOPs, managers approve controls
- support: AI drafts replies, agents ensure policy compliance

---

## 5. Common Mistakes

- giving AI ownership of high-risk outputs
- not separating draft support from final approval
- using AI where source data quality is poor
- ignoring uncertainty flags

These mistakes increase risk exposure and reduce trust.

### Artefact Title
AI Task-Risk Allocation Matrix

### What the Learner Produces
A matrix mapping at least 12 work tasks to:
- risk level
- AI role
- required human review standard

### Why It Matters
This becomes a practical operating framework for responsible AI adoption in a real role.
